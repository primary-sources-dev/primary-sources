Mission Statement

About This Project

JFK Primary Sources Archives · Established 2026

Our Mission

The assassination of President John F. Kennedy on November 22, 1963 is one of the most documented and debated events in modern American history. Decades of declassification, congressional investigations, journalistic inquiry, and public research have produced an enormous body of primary source material — and yet that material remains scattered, difficult to navigate, and inaccessible to most people.

This archive exists to change that. Our mission is to gather, organize, and present primary source materials related to the assassination in a structured, searchable, and easy-to-understand format — so that students, researchers, and the curious public can engage with the evidence directly, on their own terms.

Follow Our Progress

This archive is under active development. We regularly publish updates on new features, document collections, research progress, and technical milestones.

Editorial Position

This archive takes no position on what happened, who was responsible, or why.

The assassination remains a subject of genuine historical controversy. Official conclusions have been challenged, revised, and debated for over sixty years. Reasonable people, including credentialed historians, investigators, and researchers, hold a wide range of views.

We are not here to resolve that debate. We are here to serve it — by making the underlying source material available, organized, and legible. Every document, testimony, record, and piece of evidence presented here is a primary source. We do not editorialize, interpret, or advocate.

Where assertions are recorded in this archive, they represent claims made by sources — not conclusions drawn by this project. All assertions are linked to the specific source excerpt from which they originate. Draw your own conclusions.

How the Archive Is Organized

calendar_month Events

The archive is built around a chronological event graph. Every documented moment — from years before the assassination to the investigations that followed — is recorded as a discrete event with a timestamp, location, and participants. This lets you trace the full chain of events in order, or zoom into any single moment.

person People

Every individual named in primary sources is recorded — with their known aliases, affiliations, roles, and the events they are documented to have participated in. Start with a person and follow every thread the sources provide.

groups Organizations

Agencies, committees, companies, and groups that appear in the historical record are documented — with their memberships, roles in events, and the sources that reference them.

description Sources

Every claim in this archive traces back to a specific source — a document, testimony, audio recording, film, or physical evidence record. Sources are categorized by type, custodian, and date. Excerpts are linked directly to the assertions they support, contradict, or merely mention.

timeline Chronologies

Pre-built timelines group events into curated narrative sequences — the lead-up to Dallas, the day of the assassination, the investigation period, and more. Chronologies are reading paths, not arguments. They let you move through the record in sequence without needing to build the timeline yourself.

category Objects

Physical objects that appear in the historical record — weapons, vehicles, documents, photographs, and evidence items — are catalogued with their documented chain of custody, known locations, and the sources that reference them. Objects serve as another entry point into the evidence.

Guiding Principles

01

Primary sources only. No secondary analysis, no commentary, no opinion is introduced as fact. If it is in the archive, it is traceable to an original document or record.

02

Transparency of provenance. Every source is identified by its origin, custodian, and date. Where provenance is uncertain, that uncertainty is noted.

03

Contradictions are preserved. Where sources conflict, both accounts are recorded. The archive does not resolve disputes — it presents them.

04

Chronological structure. Information is anchored in time. Every event has a date. Where dates are uncertain, that uncertainty is explicit.

05

Accessibility without simplification. The archive is built to be navigable by anyone — student, researcher, or first-time reader — without reducing the complexity of the historical record.
